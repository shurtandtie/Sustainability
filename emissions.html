Emissions

Let's track our footprint.

Northeastern's path to reducing its carbon footprint began in 2005, when it began tracking its annual greenhouse gas emissions.


In Northeastern’s 2010 Sustainable Action Plan, the university identified carbon neutrality as its long-term goal. Using campus emission levels from 2005 as a baseline, NEU set out to reduce emissions by 20% by 2015 and pledged to further reduce emissions by 80% by 2050.[6]


On a more granular level, the plan defined emissions as metric tons of carbon dioxide equivalent per square foot of financially controlled space (MTCDE), and our website will continue to use this definition and unit of label when referring to emissions at Northeastern.


Data from Northeastern University Climate Change and Sustainability Hub from 2005 to 2022.[4]

From 2005 to 2022: Annual campus emissions

Current data indicates that total GHG emissions decreased 25% in the ten years between 2005 to 2015. This means that Northeastern went above and beyond its goals for 2015 in the 2010 Sustainable Action Plan!


Between 2015 to 2022, NEU's total GHG emissions decreased by 13%. While the university is still reducing emissions on the whole, efforts in recent years may not have been as effective as it was in the previous decade.

Analyzing NEU's emissions by scope

Let's break this down even further. When tracking our emissions, it's important to note the categories of emissions, or scopes, as they are an indicator of where the emissions are generated as well as which parties are held accountable for those emissions.

Hover over the chart to see scope 1 and scope 2 emission levels for each year!



Data from Northeastern University Climate Change and Sustainability Hub from 2005 to 2022.




Scope 1 emissions are directly caused by energy sources that an organization owns. At Northeastern University, the majority of our scope 1 emissions result from our consumption of natural gas for heating and fuel for our vehicle fleet.[3]
Scope 2 emissions are indirect emissions resulting from purchased electricity. Scope 2 emissions occur at the facility where they are generated, but we include them in our calculation of NEU’s GHG inventory because they are generated as a result of the university’s energy use.[3]


Scope 3 emissions are indirect emissions that occur because of NEU's operations, but from sources not owned or controlled by the university. Scope 3 emissions can include investments, business travel, employee commuting, and other purchased goods and services, which can be harder to keep track of and quantify. Scope 3 emissions are not included in our dataset.



What can we learn from analyzing scope 1 and scope 2 emissions independently?
While scope 2 emissions from purchased electricity have been steadily declining from 2005 to now, scope 1 emissions from campus-generated fuel sources saw an initial decrease from 2005 to 2008 but remained relatively stable in recent years.

Scope 1 emissions, which remain high today, serves as an indicator of the university's sustained use of natural gas and fuel oil. Here, we recognize the opportunity for further decrease in campus greenhouse gas emissions in years to come.





In 2017, the university experienced a prolonged power outage, resulting in a weeklong electricity outage at its residence halls.[8] Two years later, the university began the development of a microgrid to ensure more reliable power, lower electricity costs, and reduce its carbon dioxide emissions.[8]




Traditionally, electricity is generated at power plants all over the country and transported over hundreds of miles of wires to the end-users of that electricity. This process often results in line-losses, or electricity lost in travel.[7]

Between 2018 to 2022, annual electricity transmission and distribution losses averaged about 5% of the electricity transmitted and distributed in the country.[7]

Enter: microgrids. Microgrids, true to their name, are smaller, localized networks of generators that produce electricity for a specific subset of end-use customers.[1] In contrast to traditional electricity grids, microgrids serve a more local network, resulting in less electricity lost in travel, lower cost, and reduced emissions.[1]
